First, the graveyard

The operators are dead. The demand isn't.

Common · 2024

$110M+ → Chapter 7

5,200 units across 12 US cities; raised over $110M. Filed Chapter 7 in June 2024 — full liquidation, not a WeWork-style reorg. Industry autopsy: VC-driven multi-market expansion without profitability, then rates rose.

HubHaus · 2020

Master-lease trap

1,000+ rooms by 2019 — one of the largest players. It held the leases, so every unpaid room was its liability. COVID took vacancy from <5% to 30%+ while Bay Area rents fell 30%. Dead by September 2020.

Founder post-mortem (Merchant) · verified 3-0
Quarters · 2021

$300M plan, $17K left

US arm of Medici Living filed Chapter 7 with about $17,000 in the bank — and its own filings show it was unprofitable before COVID. Tenants couldn't recover their security deposits from the estate.

The Real Deal · verified 3-0
Roomi · 2018

Matching ≠ a business

The roommate-matching app laid off ~50 of 63 staff, most without severance, when a funding round fell through. Matching alone — no ops layer, no retention — couldn't carry a venture-scale company.

VentureBeat · verified 2-0

The pattern isn't "co-living failed." It's "holding the lease + VC growth targets failed."

The structural read

colive.fun sits in the surviving quadrant

Who lived? Outpost Club — ~1,500 units concentrated in 40 NYC buildings, no sprawl — absorbed roughly half of Common's inventory after the bankruptcy. Density in one market beat empire in twelve.

Their liability

Operators signed master leases and owed rent on every empty room. When vacancy spiked, the model consumed itself — at 1,000 rooms, at 5,200 units, at $300M of ambition.

Our structure

colive.fun holds no leases, custodies no rent, owns no inventory. Houses are sovereign; the platform is coordination software. The failure mode that killed four funded companies does not exist here.

Our market

NYC-only is not a limitation — it's the survivor's strategy, proven by the one operator still standing. Depth of community in one city over logos on a map.

Quick win · days, not weeks

Recommendation 1 · Quick win

A trust layer — solidarity edition

Diggz's retention weapon is verification: AI + human profile vetting, then optional ID, phone, social and reference checks, plus in-app flagging. Our directory lists anyone who registers. Adopt the layer — but build it from community fabric, not corporate ID checks.

Vouches

A member who's met you at a gathering can vouch for you. Vouches show on your profile card in Browse. The gatherings calendar already records who crossed paths.

References

Past housemates leave a short reference when someone moves out — the split protocol and leave flow are natural collection points.

Crossed-paths badge

"Seen at 3 gatherings" is organic proof-of-person no bot farms. Derived from existing RSVP data; zero new user work.

Flag + review

A report button on profiles and houses, reviewed by admins. Table stakes Diggz ships that we don't.

diggz.co trust stack · verified 3-0
Recommendation 2 · Evidence-backed

Auction mode for chores

A real commune ran a continuous chore auction and published the results: every resident owes 100 points/month; a task's value rises the longer it goes undone; claims are peer-verified by upvote. Total governance burden: 2–4 hours of chores plus one 90-minute meeting a month — and the house survived turnover.

Why it works

Neglect prices itself. The gross task nobody wants climbs in value until someone claims it — no nagging, no steward escalation, no resentment ledger.

What we have

Rotations, labor credits, bounties, mark-done. The auction is a pricing rule on top of existing rails: bounty values that accrue + peer verification instead of self-report.

How to ship it

An optional house mode next to rotation mode. Houses pick at setup or by proposal. The paper's parameters (100 pts/month, linear accrual) are sane defaults.

Recommendation 3 · Our unfair advantage

Deposits no company can take down with it

When Quarters died, tenants could not recover their security deposits from the bankrupt operator. colive.fun's gathering deposits already sit in a non-custodial Gnosis contract — no platform bankruptcy can touch them. That's not plumbing. That's the headline.

Say it

Put the custody story on the landing page and every deposit screen: "held by code, not by a company — refundable even if we disappear." The graveyard slide is the marketing copy.

Extend it

Move-in deposits for rooms: escrowed on Gnosis at join, released by the house's own vote on move-out terms. Long bet — needs UX care — but no competitor can copy it without becoming us.

The Real Deal (Quarters) · verified 2-0
Recommendation 4 · Quick win

Consent templates, not just majorities

Loomio — the tool housing co-ops, cohousing groups and ecovillages actually use — ships four decision templates: consensus, consent, majority, advice. Votes carry reasoning, and "block" is a first-class position. colive.fun has one instrument: the 2/3 vote.

Consent process

"Any meaningful objections?" — absence of objection, not presence of enthusiasm. Faster for the 90% of house decisions that are safe to try (paint the hallway, move dinner to Thursday).

Advice process

One person decides after hearing everyone — right for domains with an owner (the meal-plan steward, the tool-shed keeper). Keeps small things out of full votes.

Votes with reasons

Agree / abstain / disagree / block, each with a line of reasoning attached. The reasoning is what turns a vote count into a conversation.

Ship as proposal templates on the existing proposals rail — the 2/3 amendment vote stays for the agreement itself.

loomio.com — how it works, co-op adoption pages
Recommendation 5 · The long bet

Year five has an address: buy it together

Radish (Oakland): 20 adults, 10 kids, 8 units, co-owned since 2019 through a "FriendLLC" — with designed exit windows so people get their money out in 5–10 years. Its founder turned the playbook into Supernuclear and then a company, Live Near Friends (2023), helping groups buy property near their people.

Why us

"Built for year five, not week one" is already the pitch. A stable colive.fun house with a treasury, a signed agreement and three years of ledger history is the best-documented co-buying group in America.

First step is paper

A co-buying module in the living agreement: equity split worksheet, exit-window clauses, FriendLLC/TIC template links. Documents before deeds.

Not a pivot

We never touch the property. No brokerage, no custody, no leases — the graveyard rule holds. We give the house its paperwork and its history, they bring the mortgage.
