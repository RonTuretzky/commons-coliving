# Quiz v2 — Research Appendix

Full output of the 5-agent research swarm (2026-07-10) behind [the proposal deck](../quiz-v2-proposal.html).
Strength labels: **strong** = meta-analysis/large replication · **moderate** = solid single studies · **suggestive** = preliminary/unverified detail.

## 1 · Roommate & cohabitation conflict

The roommate literature is unambiguous about WHERE conflict comes from — cleanliness standards, noise, sleep/wake schedules, guests, and sharing/finances — and roommate conflict measurably predicts stress and academic harm, so a defensible quiz should spend its item budget on those concrete behavioral domains rather than invented typologies. The evidence splits cleanly along the main-effect/similarity/filter design question: agreeableness, conscientiousness, emotional stability, and low verbal aggressiveness are MAIN EFFECTS (good for any housemate), while defensible SIMILARITY matching is limited to concrete routines — above all sleep schedules/chronotype and calibrated cleanliness behavior — because meta-analytic and roommate-specific evidence shows actual trait similarity is a weak predictor once people actually live together, with perceived similarity doing most of the work. Measurement matters as much as constructs: uncalibrated self-labels ('I'm clean') generate false matches, so items must be frequency- and scenario-anchored behaviors. Critically, there is no rigorous evidence that questionnaire pre-matching beats random assignment, so the honest product frames the score as hard-filter screening plus expectation-setting, feeds answers into an auto-drafted house agreement (the intervention residence life actually uses), and validates itself by tracking matched households with the Roommate Relationship Scale over time. A practical stack fitting under 7 minutes: BFI-2-XS (15 items) + rMEQ (5 items) + roughly 10 behaviorally anchored domain items and hard filters (smoking, pets, guests, substances).

### Findings

- **[strong]** Roommate conflict is widespread and consequential: ~50% of college women and ~44% of men report frequent or occasional roommate conflict, frequent roommate conflict significantly predicts overall stress levels, and 5.6% of undergraduates say roommate difficulties hurt their academic performance — more than cite alcohol (4.0%).
  - *Source:* Erb, Renshaw, Short & Pollard (2014), Journal of Student Affairs Research and Practice 51(1):43-55, synthesizing Liu, Sharkness & Pryor (2008, n=31,500), Dusselier et al. (2005, J. American College Health, n=416), and ACHA-NCHA (2012). Verified from full-text PDF.
  - *Design implication:* The quiz is addressing a real, high-base-rate, high-stakes outcome; optimize it for conflict prevention in concrete domains, not for producing an entertaining personality typology.
- **[moderate]** The same behavioral domains dominate roommate conflict across studies: cleanliness standards, noise, sleep/wake schedules, guests/visitors, and sharing/borrowing of space and belongings (the Erb review names noise level, sleep/waking hours, and visitors as the core negotiated domains; open-ended complaints center on habits like 'coming home late and waking them').
  - *Source:* Converging descriptive studies: Erb et al. (2014) JSARP review; Dusselier, Dunn, Wang, Shelley & Whalen (2005); multiple dorm-conflict surveys (e.g., MSU Journal of Undergraduate Research). No single meta-analysis exists.
  - *Design implication:* The quiz's item budget should go primarily to these five concrete domains (cleanliness, noise, sleep, guests, sharing/finances) rather than to abstract invented dimensions like Hearth/Order/Voice.
- **[strong]** Meta-analytically, ACTUAL similarity predicts attraction only in zero-acquaintance lab paradigms and largely stops predicting outcomes in existing relationships, whereas PERCEIVED similarity predicts liking and satisfaction in both.
  - *Source:* Montoya, Horton & Kirchner (2008), 'Is actual similarity necessary for attraction? A meta-analysis of actual and perceived similarity,' Journal of Social and Personal Relationships; companion process meta-analysis Montoya & Horton (2013), JSPR (2013 paper verified in search results; 2008 details from prior knowledge).
  - *Design implication:* A match % built mostly on trait-similarity scoring is on weak footing; similarity scoring should be reserved for concrete, behavioral routines, and the product should also actively build perceived similarity (e.g., surfacing genuine commonalities to matched pairs).
- **[moderate]** In 103 pairs of newly acquainted college roommates tracked over a semester, Big Five trait similarity was not the driver of relationship quality; instead, quality tracked individual standing and perception — good relationships went with seeing one's roommate as agreeable, conscientious, extraverted, and low in neuroticism, with conscientiousness positively tied to relationship quality.
  - *Source:* Kurtz & Sherker (2003), 'Relationship quality, trait similarity, and self-other agreement on personality ratings in college roommates,' Journal of Personality 71(1). Verified via PubMed/Wiley abstract.
  - *Design implication:* Treat agreeableness, conscientiousness, and emotional stability as MAIN EFFECTS (screen/score everyone on them) rather than as similarity-matching dimensions.
- **[moderate]** In roommate dyads specifically, perceived similarity is more strongly associated with satisfaction and closeness than actual similarity — partly because self-report labels are uncalibrated: two roommates can both rate themselves 'willing to keep the room clean' while holding very different standards, which is itself a documented conflict generator.
  - *Source:* Niu & Brown (2023), 'Satisfaction and closeness in same- and cross-national college roommate relationships: What similarity matters?', International Journal of Intercultural Relations (authors/venue verified via Crossref, DOI 10.1016/j.ijintrel.2023.101813); full effect details paywalled, so partially verified.
  - *Design implication:* Use behaviorally anchored, frequency-based items ('dishes washed within 24 hours', 'overnight guests ~N nights/month', 'typical lights-out time') instead of agree/disagree self-labels or forced-choice identity items — self-labels don't calibrate across people.
- **[moderate]** Among 150 cohabiting couples, mismatched sleep/wake patterns (a morning person living with an evening person) predicted significantly worse adjustment, more conflict, less serious conversation, and less shared activity than matched couples — and sleep-schedule/noise issues are simultaneously a top-3 roommate conflict source.
  - *Source:* Larson, Crane & Smith (1991), 'Morning and night couples: The effect of wake and sleep patterns on marital adjustment,' Journal of Marital and Family Therapy 17(1):53-65. Verified via Wiley/ERIC.
  - *Design implication:* Chronotype/sleep schedule is one of the few genuinely defensible SIMILARITY dimensions; ask actual typical bed/wake times and weight schedule mismatch heavily, especially for shared-room or thin-wall living situations.
- **[suggestive]** There is no rigorous evidence that questionnaire-based pre-matching improves roommate outcomes over random assignment: institutional data (e.g., UW-Madison housing surveys) and Brown University's experience find randomly assigned roommates report satisfaction equal to or better than self-selected/preference-matched ones, and no published RCT shows preference matching beats random pairing.
  - *Source:* UW-Madison University Housing assignment data; Brown University (2024) institutional report on random first-year assignment; absence-of-evidence claim from my own literature search. Institutional/journalistic, not peer-reviewed — hence marked suggestive.
  - *Design implication:* Be epistemically humble about the match %: present quiz output as (a) hard-filter screening, (b) expectation-setting, and (c) structured conversation input — not as a validated compatibility probability; and instrument Commons to collect outcome data so matching can be validated internally.
- **[suggestive]** Roommate agreements/behavior contracts are the most widely institutionalized conflict-prevention tool in residence life and are supported by preliminary research as a way to surface expectations before conflict, but formal efficacy evidence remains thin (a preliminary study, not an RCT).
  - *Source:* McCorkle & Mason (2009), 'Conflict in Residence Halls: A Preliminary Study of the Efficacy of Roommate Negotiations to Reduce Roommate Conflict,' Boise State ScholarWorks (conference proceedings). Verified authors/year via repository page.
  - *Design implication:* Pipe quiz answers directly into an auto-drafted 'house agreement' (quiet hours, cleaning cadence, guest norms, cost splits) that matched housemates review together — the quiz's biggest defensible value may be as an agreement generator, which also fits Commons' existing House Ledger/check-in machinery.
- **[moderate]** Housemates become emotionally interdependent over time: cognitive vulnerability to depression was 'contagious' between randomly assigned freshman roommates over 3-6 months, and roommate pairs show emotional convergence over 9 months of living together.
  - *Source:* Haeffel & Hames (2014), Clinical Psychological Science (103 randomly assigned pairs) and Anderson, Keltner & John (2003), both verified as summarized in Erb et al. (2014) JSARP review Table 1.
  - *Design implication:* Emotional stability and wellbeing habits are legitimate MAIN-EFFECT signals (they spill over onto housemates), and the platform should build in post-move-in wellbeing check-ins rather than treating matching as one-and-done.
- **[suggestive]** In 30 randomly assigned first-year roommate pairs, similarity on 'interpersonal adequacy' (social skills/leadership) and 'character' (maturity, responsibility) predicted greater relationship satisfaction — early evidence that some deep similarity helps, but from a very small sample.
  - *Source:* Carli, Ganley & Pierce-Otay (1991), 'Similarity and satisfaction in roommate relationships,' Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin 17(4). Verified via SAGE. Marked suggestive due to n=30 pairs.
  - *Design implication:* A modest similarity component on maturity/responsibility-adjacent behaviors is defensible as a secondary signal, but should never dominate the main-effect and hard-filter layers.
- **[suggestive]** Roommate pairs who are similar in communication traits — both willing to communicate, both competent, and both LOW in verbal aggressiveness — report the highest satisfaction and liking; low verbal aggressiveness functions as a main effect while communication style works as a similarity effect.
  - *Source:* Widely cited roommate communication-trait study, attributed to Martin & Anderson (mid-1990s, communication journals); I could not fully verify author/year within search budget, so marked suggestive.
  - *Design implication:* Include 2-3 items on conflict approach ('I raise issues directly within a few days' vs 'I avoid confrontation') — score direct/low-aggression styles as a positive main effect and style similarity as a secondary match signal.
- **[moderate]** Roommate dissolution has social-identity drivers independent of habits: in a naturalistic study of randomly assigned dorm pairs, interracial roommate relationships dissolved at higher rates and were less successful on average than same-race pairs, even though sustained interracial contact improved intergroup attitudes.
  - *Source:* Shook & Fazio (2008), 'Success of interracial versus same race roommate relationships' (existence, authors verified via Crossref; details from prior knowledge of the Ohio State studies, incl. Shook & Fazio 2008, Psychological Science on attitude change).
  - *Design implication:* Do NOT match on demographics; instead invest in the conditions that make diverse households succeed (structured agreements, early check-ins, shared activities) — and expect the quiz alone cannot explain all dissolution.

### Usable instruments

- **BFI-2-XS (Big Five Inventory-2 Extra-Short Form; Soto & John 2017, Journal of Research in Personality)** — 15 items, 5-point Likert (about 90 seconds). Construct: Big Five domains — of which Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Negative Emotionality are the housemate-relevant main effects. Validated, free, short enough to fit a 7-minute quiz; supplies the main-effect layer (Kurtz & Sherker: individual conscientiousness/agreeableness beat trait similarity) with real psychometrics instead of invented dimensions.
- **TIPI (Ten-Item Personality Inventory; Gosling, Rentfrow & Swann 2003, Journal of Research in Personality)** — 10 items, 7-point Likert (about 60 seconds). Construct: Big Five, ultra-brief. Fallback if the item budget is extremely tight; widely used and normed, though reliability is lower than BFI-2-XS — use only if the 5 extra BFI-2-XS items truly cannot fit.
- **rMEQ (reduced Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire; Adan & Almirall 1991, Personality and Individual Differences)** — 5 multiple-choice items (about 60 seconds). Construct: Chronotype (morningness-eveningness). Validated 5-item chronotype measure powering the strongest defensible SIMILARITY match (sleep-schedule alignment, per Larson et al. 1991 and top-3 conflict-source data); pair it with one behavioral item on actual weekday bed/wake times.
- **Roommate Relationship Scale (RRS; West 2008; psychometrically validated by Willis & Lane 2022, Frontiers in Psychology)** — 17-19 items, 5- or 7-point Likert; single factor of perceived roommate relationship quality. Construct: Roommate relationship quality (outcome measure). Not for the intake quiz — use it as the criterion: administer to matched households at 1/3/6 months so Commons can empirically validate and tune its own matching algorithm, which the field currently lacks.
- **Verbal Aggressiveness Scale, short form (Infante & Wigley 1986, Communication Monographs)** — 20 items original; 10-item short form in common research use; 5-point Likert. Construct: Trait verbal aggressiveness in conflict. Low verbal aggressiveness is a main-effect predictor of roommate satisfaction; even 3-4 adapted items add a communication-style signal — though items are face-transparent, so treat scores as advisory (susceptible to socially desirable responding).

## 2 · Personality science for shared living

The personality evidence points to a clear architecture: MAIN EFFECTS dominate. Large representative-sample research (Dyrenforth et al. 2010) and roommate-specific studies (Kurtz & Sherker 2003) show that living with someone high in agreeableness, conscientiousness, and emotional stability predicts satisfaction for everyone, while global personality-similarity adds almost nothing once those levels are accounted for — and Montoya et al.'s (2008) meta-analysis shows actual similarity stops predicting liking in ongoing relationships (perceived similarity is what matters, which is a UI problem, not a scoring problem). HEXACO Honesty-Humility deserves a place as the strongest trait predictor of non-exploitation (freeriding on chores, bills, shared resources; Zettler et al. 2020 meta-analytic net). SIMILARITY earns a genuine but narrow role in two behavioral domains: tidiness standards (cleanliness is the top reported roommate conflict source) and sleep schedules, where chronotype/bedtime mismatch is linked to more conflict (Larson et al. 1991; Gordon & Chen 2014; sleep-schedule items can work as soft or hard filters). Practically, a defensible sub-7-minute quiz is BFI-2-S or BFI-2-XS (Big Five main effects) + HEXACO-60 Honesty-Humility (10 items) + rMEQ (5 items, chronotype matching) + a handful of concrete tidiness/noise standards items, replacing the invented six dimensions. Finally, 2-item-per-trait scales (TIPI, BFI-10) are too unreliable for person-level match decisions, so scores should be presented as coarse bands rather than a precise match percentage.

### Findings

- **[strong]** In three nationally representative couple samples (Australia, UK, Germany; ~23,000 couples), one's own and one's partner's Big Five levels predicted relationship satisfaction, while personality-similarity effects were near zero (<0.5% of variance) and inconsistent across samples once main effects were controlled.
  - *Source:* Dyrenforth, Kashy, Donnellan & Lucas (2010), Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 99(4) — verified via PubMed 20718544
  - *Design implication:* The core scoring model should weight each person's ABSOLUTE levels of agreeableness, conscientiousness, and emotional stability (main effects), not profile likeness; a match % computed purely from trait-distance is scientifically indefensible.
- **[strong]** In the same large-sample study, partner effects were largest for agreeableness, conscientiousness, and emotional stability — living with someone high on these traits predicts YOUR satisfaction regardless of your own profile.
  - *Source:* Dyrenforth et al. (2010), JPSP; converging with Malouff et al.'s partner-satisfaction meta-analytic tradition (not independently verified this session)
  - *Design implication:* Treat A, C, and emotional stability as universal 'good housemate' signals: score them for everyone and surface them symmetrically to both parties, rather than folding them into a similarity metric.
- **[strong]** Meta-analysis of 313 studies (460 effect sizes) found actual similarity predicts attraction only in no-interaction lab paradigms; in existing, ongoing relationships actual similarity is essentially non-predictive while PERCEIVED similarity remains predictive (r ≈ .39).
  - *Source:* Montoya, Horton & Kirchner (2008), Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, 25(6), 889-922 — verified
  - *Design implication:* Don't oversell trait-likeness matching; instead design the results UI to build perceived similarity (highlight verified commonalities in values and lifestyle), which is the mechanism that actually sustains liking once people live together.
- **[strong]** A large-scale meta-analytic 'nomological net' (426 meta-analyses, 436 samples) shows HEXACO Honesty-Humility uniquely predicts (low) exploitation — cheating, deviance, freeriding — and cooperation even when exploitation is possible, beyond what Big Five agreeableness captures.
  - *Source:* Zettler, Thielmann, Hilbig & Moshagen (2020), Perspectives on Psychological Science, 15(3) — verified
  - *Design implication:* Add Honesty-Humility as a MAIN-EFFECT screen: it is the best trait predictor of whether someone will free-ride on bills, chores, and shared food — arguably the most housemate-relevant construct the current quiz doesn't touch.
- **[moderate]** In 103 previously unacquainted college roommate dyads (NEO-FFI), relationship quality was tied to seeing one's roommate as agreeable, conscientious, extraverted, and low in neuroticism, with perceived (self-rated) similarity showing more consistent effects than objectively measured similarity.
  - *Source:* Kurtz & Sherker (2003), Journal of Personality, 71(1) — verified via PubMed 12597236 and Wiley
  - *Design implication:* Roommate-specific data mirrors the couples data: prioritize the roommate's A/C/low-N levels; use similarity displays to shape perception rather than as the underlying scoring engine.
- **[moderate]** Among randomly assigned college roommates, greater similarity was associated with higher roommate satisfaction — the classic evidence that likeness matters somewhat in forced shared living.
  - *Source:* Carli, Ganley & Pierce-Otay (1991), Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 17(4), 419-426 — verified via OpenAlex; note: a circulating 'Carli 2019, r=.41 conscientiousness similarity' citation appears only on blogs and could NOT be verified — do not cite it
  - *Design implication:* Similarity earns a secondary role in the algorithm (lifestyle/values items), but the widely quoted r=.41 conscientiousness-similarity figure is likely fabricated and should not appear in Commons marketing or docs.
- **[suggestive]** Reviews of college roommate research consistently identify cleanliness and noise from differing living habits as the largest sources of roommate conflict.
  - *Source:* Erb, Renshaw, Short & Pollard (2014), 'The Importance of College Roommate Relationships' (review; PDF hosted at George Mason) — title/authors seen, venue not fully verified this session, so marked suggestive
  - *Design implication:* The two domains where MISMATCH demonstrably drives conflict are tidiness standards and noise/sleep — these deserve concrete behavioral-standards items (e.g., 'dishes done within X') scored for similarity or as soft filters, unlike broad traits.
- **[moderate]** Chronotype-mismatched ('morning vs night') couples reported worse marital adjustment, more conflict, and less shared time than matched couples.
  - *Source:* Larson, Crane & Smith (1991), 'Morning and night couples: The effect of wake and sleep patterns on marital adjustment', Journal of Marital and Family Therapy — verified via multiple secondary citations
  - *Design implication:* Chronotype is a SIMILARITY/soft-filter dimension: match on morningness-eveningness (or at least flag large mismatches) rather than treating it as a trait where more is better.
- **[moderate]** Daily-diary and lab data show poor sleep predicts more next-day relationship conflict and worse conflict resolution; resolution was best when both parties were well rested.
  - *Source:* Gordon & Chen (2014), 'The role of sleep in interpersonal conflict', Social Psychological and Personality Science, 5(2) — verified
  - *Design implication:* Sleep protection is a conflict-prevention mechanism, so quiz items about quiet hours, guest noise, and light/noise sensitivity are functionally conflict-risk items, not lifestyle trivia.
- **[suggestive]** In 234 middle-aged couples, greater differences in partners' bedtimes were associated with higher conflict frequency for both partners (mediated by attachment avoidance).
  - *Source:* Novak & Wilson (2020), Innovation in Aging (GSA conference abstract, PMC7743770) — conference abstract, not a peer-reviewed full paper, hence suggestive
  - *Design implication:* Reinforces using bedtime/wake-time gap as a mismatch penalty in scoring; a simple reported-bedtime item may capture this more directly than chronotype alone.
- **[strong]** Ultra-short Big Five scales trade reliability for brevity: BFI-10 inter-item pair correlations run .12 (agreeableness) to .38 (neuroticism) with documented factorial-validity problems, and both BFI-10 and TIPI authors warn against individual-level diagnostic use; test-retest is more acceptable (~.72-.75).
  - *Source:* Rammstedt & John (2007), Journal of Research in Personality 41 (BFI-10); Gosling, Rentfrow & Swann (2003), JRP 37 (TIPI); replication caveats in Hogrefe/EJPA 2023 BFI-10 structural analyses — verified
  - *Design implication:* If Commons makes person-level match decisions, 2-items-per-trait scales are too noisy; use at least 3 items per domain (BFI-2-XS) and preferably 6 (BFI-2-S, ~3 minutes), and present outputs as coarse bands, not precise percentages.
- **[strong]** The 5-item reduced Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire (rMEQ) validly classifies chronotype with good test-retest reliability and cross-national validation, and is recommended precisely for screening when questionnaire length must be minimized.
  - *Source:* Adan & Almirall (1991), Personality and Individual Differences 12 (derived from Horne & Östberg 1976 MEQ); psychometrics confirmed in Danielsson et al. (2019), Chronobiology International — verified
  - *Design implication:* rMEQ gives a defensible chronotype measure for ~30 seconds of quiz time; use its standard cutoffs (evening ≤11, morning ≥18) to drive the sleep-mismatch penalty.

### Usable instruments

- **BFI-2-S (Big Five Inventory-2 Short Form)** — 30 items, 5-point Likert (~2.5-3 minutes). Construct: Big Five domains (6 items each) plus rough facet coverage. Best validity-per-minute option: Soto & John (2017, JRP 68) report it retains ~90% of the full BFI-2's reliability and validity at domain level — reliable enough for person-level matching decisions, unlike 10-item scales; verified citation.
- **BFI-2-XS (Extra-Short Form)** — 15 items, 5-point Likert (~90 seconds). Construct: Big Five domains only (3 items each). The floor for defensible individual-level Big Five measurement if the time budget is very tight; retains roughly three-quarters of full-form validity but facet information and per-user precision degrade — pair with coarse output bands (Soto & John 2017, verified).
- **HEXACO-60 Honesty-Humility subscale** — 10 items, 5-point Likert (~1 minute); alpha ~.74-.79 in the HEXACO-60. Construct: Honesty-Humility (sincerity, fairness, greed avoidance, modesty). Captures the exploitation/freeriding dimension (bills, chores, shared food) that Big Five scales miss; Ashton & Lee (2009, Journal of Personality Assessment 91:4, verified) plus the Zettler et al. (2020) meta-analytic evidence make it the single strongest addition to a housemate screen.
- **rMEQ (reduced Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire)** — 5 multiple-choice items (~30-45 seconds), summed score 4-25; evening type ≤11, morning type ≥18. Construct: Chronotype (morningness-eveningness). Validated across many countries with good test-retest reliability (Adan & Almirall 1991, verified); feeds a sleep-schedule mismatch penalty, which is one of the two domains where dissimilarity demonstrably drives cohabitation conflict.
- **TIPI (Ten-Item Personality Inventory)** — 10 items, 7-point Likert (~1 minute). Construct: Big Five domains (2 items each). Fallback only: Gosling, Rentfrow & Swann (2003, verified) deliberately sacrificed internal consistency (alphas .40-.73) for breadth; acceptable for aggregate analytics or coarse tie-breaking but the authors themselves caution against individual diagnostic use — BFI-2-XS is better at similar length.

## 3 · Matching-algorithm validity

The evidence on questionnaire-based compatibility matching is unusually consistent: pre-meeting self-reports can predict individual main effects (who is generally easy to live with — roughly 4-27% of actor/partner variance in Joel et al. 2017; up to ~21% from individual differences in Joel et al. 2020) but predict essentially zero dyad-specific 'chemistry' variance, and Finkel et al.'s 2012 PSPI monograph found no matching algorithm with demonstrated validity. Similarity matching in particular is weakly supported for real cohabitation: Montoya et al.'s 2008 meta-analysis shows actual similarity predicts attraction only in no-interaction paradigms and is non-significant in existing relationships, and Eastwick et al. 2014 show matching on people's stated ideals fails for abstract traits in live contexts. What survives scrutiny is (a) non-compensatory hard filters — Jonason et al. 2015 show negative information is weighted asymmetrically, so dealbreakers should gate matches regardless of other scores — and (b) main-effect trait screening (Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Emotional Stability per roommate studies), plus concrete behavioral-logistics alignment. A single 'match %' therefore conflates a defensible layer with an indefensible one, and the OkCupid 2014 experiment shows displayed percentages are causally self-fulfilling, creating an ethical duty not to display false precision. Recommended redesign: layered output (dealbreaker check → lifestyle-logistics alignment → individual housemate-readiness) with generated house-interview prompts instead of a verdict, and honest copy that pairwise fit can only be discovered by meeting. Since Joel et al. 2020 show relationship-specific experience carries most of the variance, Commons's existing weekly check-ins — not the intake quiz — are the scientifically correct place to measure and improve actual compatibility over time.

### Findings

- **[strong]** A comprehensive review of online dating found no compelling evidence that any proprietary questionnaire-based matching algorithm (eHarmony, Chemistry.com, etc.) predicts relationship success better than chance or self-selection; sites' claims rested on unpublished, uncontrolled internal studies, and algorithms focus on similarity in traits/attitudes precisely because it is easy to measure pre-match, not because it predicts outcomes.
  - *Source:* Finkel, Eastwick, Karney, Reis & Sprecher (2012), 'Online Dating: A Critical Analysis From the Perspective of Psychological Science', Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 13(1), 3-66. Verified.
  - *Design implication:* Do not market the quiz as predicting long-term co-living success. Frame it as a structured screening and conversation tool. Any 'match %' derived from pre-meeting questionnaires is scientifically indefensible as a dyadic outcome prediction.
- **[strong]** Machine learning (random forests) applied to 100+ self-report measures before speed dates predicted 4-18% of actor variance (one's general tendency to desire others) and 7-27% of partner variance (one's general tendency to be desired), but 0% of relationship variance — the dyad-specific 'chemistry' component — establishing a hard ceiling on pre-meeting compatibility prediction.
  - *Source:* Joel, Eastwick & Finkel (2017), 'Is Romantic Desire Predictable? Machine Learning Applied to Initial Romantic Attraction', Psychological Science, 28(10), 1478-1489. Verified.
  - *Design implication:* The quiz CAN validly measure main effects — who will generally be an easy, well-liked housemate — and CANNOT measure pairwise chemistry. Score people on individual 'good housemate' traits plus hard filters; never present a pairwise chemistry prediction.
- **[strong]** Across 43 longitudinal datasets (11,196 couples), one's own relationship-specific perceptions explained up to 45% of relationship-quality variance, own individual-difference traits up to 21%, while the PARTNER'S reports and traits added essentially no predictive power beyond one's own reports; change in relationship quality over time was largely unpredictable from any self-report combination.
  - *Source:* Joel et al. (2020), 'Machine learning uncovers the most robust self-report predictors of relationship quality across 43 longitudinal couples studies', PNAS, 117(32), 19061-19071. Verified against the paper's significance statement (PDF).
  - *Design implication:* The real signal lives in relationship-specific experience AFTER people live together, not in pre-match trait profiles of the other person. Pair a lightweight intake quiz with recurring post-move-in check-ins (which Commons already has) — the check-in data, not the intake quiz, is where compatibility is actually measurable.
- **[strong]** Meta-analysis of 313 studies (460 effect sizes) found actual similarity strongly predicts attraction only in no-interaction lab paradigms (r = .47); its effect shrinks with real interaction and is NOT significant in existing relationships, whereas perceived similarity (r = .39) predicts attraction in all contexts including established relationships.
  - *Source:* Montoya, Horton & Kirchner (2008), 'Is actual similarity necessary for attraction? A meta-analysis of actual and perceived similarity', Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, 25(6), 889-922. Verified.
  - *Design implication:* Do not build the match score primarily on trait-similarity distance metrics — actual similarity mostly predicts first impressions, not living-together satisfaction. Perceived similarity is largely a byproduct of a good relationship. Weight similarity modestly and only on concrete lifestyle logistics, not personality profiles.
- **[strong]** Meta-analytic review found that stated ideal-partner preferences predict evaluations of hypothetical profiles but the match between a person's ideals and a live partner's actual traits fails to predict their evaluations in face-to-face contexts.
  - *Source:* Eastwick, Luchies, Finkel & Hunt (2014), 'The Predictive Validity of Ideal Partner Preferences: A Review and Meta-Analysis', Psychological Bulletin, 140(3), 623-665. Verified.
  - *Design implication:* Asking users what they WANT in a housemate and matching on it is weakly valid for abstract traits (e.g., 'warm', 'fun'). Instead ask about their OWN concrete behaviors (cleaning frequency, guest habits, sleep schedule) and use others' preferences only for concrete, verifiable logistics.
- **[strong]** Across six studies (N > 6,500), people weighed dealbreakers (negative traits/behaviors) more heavily than they weighed equivalent dealmakers positively — a negativity/error-management asymmetry — and dealbreakers mattered more in long-term relationship contexts.
  - *Source:* Jonason, Garcia, Webster, Li & Fisher (2015), 'Relationship Dealbreakers: Traits People Avoid in Potential Mates', Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 41(12), 1697-1711. Verified. (Romantic/friendship contexts, not housemates specifically.)
  - *Design implication:* Hard filters must be a non-compensatory layer: one violated dealbreaker (smoking, pets, cleanliness floor, overnight guests, substances) should gate or heavily flag a match regardless of how similar the pair scores elsewhere. Never let a high overall % wash out a dealbreaker conflict; surface conflicts explicitly.
- **[suggestive]** OkCupid's 'power of suggestion' experiment showed the displayed match percentage is causally self-fulfilling: telling true 30%-match pairs they were 90% matches increased first messages and conversation continuation nearly as much as genuine algorithmic compatibility ('the mere myth of compatibility works almost as well as the truth').
  - *Source:* Rudder (2014), 'We Experiment On Human Beings!', OkTrends/OkCupid company blog; widely reported (Forbes, NPR, 2014). Not peer-reviewed — a company-run randomized experiment — so marked suggestive despite large N.
  - *Design implication:* A displayed percentage is a nudge, not a neutral report — users will act on whatever number you show. That creates an ethical obligation not to display precise-looking percentages the science cannot support; instead show categorical output (dealbreaker conflicts, genuine shared ground, discussion prompts) and harness expectancy honestly by highlighting real common ground.
- **[moderate]** In 103 randomly-paired, previously unacquainted college roommate dyads, better roommate relationship quality was associated with the partner's Big Five standing — higher other-rated Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Conscientiousness and lower Neuroticism — i.e., main-effect 'easy to live with' traits rather than trait similarity.
  - *Source:* Kurtz & Sherker (2003), 'Relationship Quality, Trait Similarity, and Self-Other Agreement on Personality Ratings in College Roommates', Journal of Personality, 71(1). Verified (single study, female-only sample, correlational).
  - *Design implication:* Include a brief Big Five measure and score Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Emotional Stability as main-effect 'good housemate for anyone' signals — this is the questionnaire content with the best evidence base for cohabitation specifically.
- **[suggestive]** An early study of randomly assigned college roommates (30 pairs, plus a follow-up sample) reported that roommate similarity was associated with higher satisfaction, suggesting similarity effects may operate somewhat more in forced cohabitation than in romantic attraction — but samples were small.
  - *Source:* Carli, Ganley & Pierce-Otay (1991), 'Similarity and Satisfaction in Roommate Relationships', Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 17(4), 419-426. Citation verified but I could not verify effect details/sizes from the abstract, so marked suggestive.
  - *Design implication:* A modest similarity component (especially lifestyle-level: schedules, tidiness standards, socializing at home) is defensible as a secondary signal in a housemate context, but should carry far less weight than hard filters and main-effect traits.
- **[strong]** Synthesis across the above: what pre-meeting questionnaires CAN validly deliver is (a) hard-incompatibility detection on concrete logistics and (b) main-effect trait screening; what they CANNOT deliver is dyad-specific chemistry prediction — so any single 'match %' number conflates a defensible layer with an indefensible one.
  - *Source:* Synthesis of Finkel et al. 2012 (PSPI), Joel et al. 2017 (Psych Science), Joel et al. 2020 (PNAS), Montoya et al. 2008 (JSPR) — all individually verified.
  - *Design implication:* Replace the single match % with a layered, honestly-labeled output: (1) 'Dealbreaker check' (pass/conflict list, non-compensatory), (2) 'Lifestyle logistics alignment' (concrete behaviors: sleep, guests, cleanliness, noise, sharing), (3) 'Housemate readiness' (individual main-effect traits, same for all pairings), plus explicit copy that chemistry can only be discovered by meeting — e.g., generate discussion prompts for a house interview rather than a verdict.

### Usable instruments

- **BFI-2-XS (Big Five Inventory-2 Extra-Short Form)** — 15 items, 5-point Likert (about 2 minutes). Construct: Big Five: Extraversion, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Negative Emotionality, Open-Mindedness (3 items each). Soto & John (2017, Journal of Research in Personality) validated short form. Covers the main-effect traits with the best cohabitation evidence (Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, low Neuroticism per Kurtz & Sherker 2003) plus Extraversion for modest lifestyle-similarity scoring. Free for research/noncommercial use; better psychometrics than TIPI at 15 items.
- **TIPI (Ten-Item Personality Inventory)** — 10 items, 7-point Likert (about 1 minute). Construct: Big Five, 2 items per trait. Gosling, Rentfrow & Swann (2003, JRP). The fastest defensible Big Five measure if the 7-minute budget is tight; weaker reliability than BFI-2-XS, so use only if the item budget forces it.
- **ECR-RS (Experiences in Close Relationships - Relationship Structures), global version** — 9 items, 7-point Likert (about 1.5 minutes). Construct: Attachment anxiety and attachment avoidance. Fraley, Heffernan, Vicary & Brumbaugh (2011, Psychological Assessment). Attachment avoidance and anxiety were among the top individual-difference predictors of relationship quality in Joel et al. 2020 (PNAS). Optional module — may read as clinical for a housemate product; if used, use for coaching/expectation-setting content, not gating.
- **Housemate dealbreaker checklist modeled on Jonason et al. (2015)** — Roughly 15-20 select-all-that-apply binary items plus a 'which of these are absolute for you' second pass. Construct: Non-compensatory hard filters: smoking/vaping, pets, overnight guests, noise/quiet hours, cleanliness floor, substances, temperature, food sharing, subletting, etc.. Jonason et al. (2015, PSPB) methodology: elicit dealbreakers separately from preferences because negative information is weighted asymmetrically. This is the layer of the quiz with the strongest scientific license — hard incompatibilities are the one thing questionnaires demonstrably catch. Domain items must be adapted and piloted (the original list is for mates, not housemates).
- **SWLS (Satisfaction With Life Scale)** — 5 items, 7-point Likert (under 1 minute). Construct: Global life satisfaction. Diener, Emmons, Larsen & Griffin (1985, Journal of Personality Assessment). Life satisfaction was the single top individual-difference predictor of relationship quality in Joel et al. 2020. Ethically it should NOT gate matching (screening out unhappy people is discriminatory and self-undermining); use it for internal validation research — i.e., testing whether your quiz scores actually predict later house-ledger/check-in outcomes.

## 4 · Intentional-community longevity

The community-longevity literature converges on an uncomfortable point for a matching quiz: survival is predicted less by interpersonal chemistry than by shared purpose, accepted costly commitment, and governance clarity — Zablocki even found that denser 'loving' ties predicted MORE turnover. Sosis & Bressler's 83-commune analysis shows ideologically unified communes outlasted secular ones roughly 3-to-1 in annual survival odds, and that costly requirements extended life only when anchored to shared meaning — so a secular co-living product should match people on commitment tier and mission appetite rather than maximizing rules. The strongest evidence (Cox et al.'s 91-study review of Ostrom's principles) concerns properties of the household institution, not the individuals: clear boundaries, participatory rule-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions, and cheap conflict resolution — things the product can generate (a starter house charter from quiz answers) rather than merely measure. Practitioner data (Christian's ~90% failure estimate; FIC screening norms) attribute most failures to structural conflict from undefined agreements and decision processes, making decision-style agreement a hard filter, not an averaged dimension. Finally, every long-lived real community screens behaviorally — visit, trial stay, provisional membership — so the quiz should be framed as the front-end to a scaffolded trial with a built-in mutual review, and its match score validated against a real outcome measure like the 8-item Brief Sense of Community Scale. Net design guidance: measure willingness-to-commit (main effect), commitment tier + mission appetite + process tolerance (similarity), obligation dealbreakers (hard filters), and spend product surface area on agreements and trial structure, which the evidence says matter more than the match % itself.

### Findings

- **[moderate]** Among 83 19th-century US communes, secular communes were about 3x more likely to dissolve in any given year than religious/ideologically unified ones, and religious communes imposed more than twice as many costly requirements on members (mean 7.0 vs 3.0).
  - *Source:* Sosis & Bressler (2003), 'Cooperation and Commune Longevity: A Test of the Costly Signaling Theory of Religion', Cross-Cultural Research 37(2) — verified against full text (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1069397103037002003)
  - *Design implication:* Shared purpose is a longevity predictor, not decor. The quiz should measure how much each person wants the house to have an explicit shared mission (and what kind) — this is both a SIMILARITY dimension (mission-driven people should live together) and a HARD FILTER (mission-driven person + 'it's just an apartment' person is a known failure mode).
- **[moderate]** In the same dataset, the NUMBER of costly requirements (food taboos, labor quotas, resource pooling, guest restrictions, etc.) predicted longevity for religious communes but NOT for secular ones — in the interaction model, requirement count fully absorbed the religion effect, and a polynomial trend suggests the highest-requirement communes did not survive longest (inverted-U).
  - *Source:* Sosis & Bressler (2003), Cross-Cultural Research, Table 4 and note 9 — verified against full-text PDF (cognitionandculture.net)
  - *Design implication:* Piling on house rules alone won't create cohesion in a secular co-living product; obligations only bind when anchored to a shared 'why'. Match on COMMITMENT LEVEL (how many concrete obligations — shared meals, labor hours, meetings — a person will happily accept) as a similarity dimension, and pair rule-heavy houses with an articulated purpose. Don't reward 'maximum rules' in scoring.
- **[moderate]** Comparative-historical analysis of ~30 19th-century American utopian communities found that the long-lived ones ('successful' = 25-33+ years) systematically used more commitment mechanisms — sacrifice, investment, renunciation, communion, mortification, transcendence — than short-lived ones.
  - *Source:* Kanter (1972), 'Commitment and Community: Communes and Utopias in Sociological Perspective', Harvard University Press — book and thesis verified; exact commune counts from memory, so treat specific Ns cautiously
  - *Design implication:* MAIN EFFECT: willingness to invest (time, money, effort, participation in rituals like weekly dinners) predicts staying power regardless of who the housemates are. Include 2-3 items measuring concrete willingness-to-invest, and have the product ask for small real investments at match time (deposit, signed agreement, attending a house dinner) rather than making joining frictionless.
- **[moderate]** In Zablocki's landmark study of contemporary American communes (120 communes incl. 60 urban, longitudinal), higher density of dyadic 'loving' relationships within a commune predicted GREATER membership turnover and instability, not less.
  - *Source:* Zablocki (1980), 'Alienation and Charisma: A Study of Contemporary American Communes', Free Press, pp. 155-156 — finding verified via secondary scholarly sources, not the primary text
  - *Design implication:* Interpersonal chemistry/'vibe' is not a survival predictor and intense emotional enmeshment is a risk factor. Weight practical and values alignment over affinity questions ('would you be friends?'), and do not market the match score as friendship chemistry — frame it as household viability.
- **[strong]** A review of 91 empirical commons-governance cases found Ostrom's design principles well supported: clearly defined membership boundaries, rules congruent with local conditions, participation in rule-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions, and cheap accessible conflict-resolution mechanisms characterize groups that successfully sustain shared resources.
  - *Source:* Cox, Arnold & Villamayor-Tomás (2010), 'A Review of Design Principles for Community-based Natural Resource Management', Ecology and Society 15(4):38 — verified; note the evidence base is natural-resource commons, extrapolation to households is an inference
  - *Design implication:* The strongest-evidence lever is GOVERNANCE CLARITY, which is a property of the house, not the person. The product should (a) measure each person's comfort with explicit agreements, accountability, and graduated consequences (main effect items), and (b) push every matched house to adopt written agreements, a rule-change process, and a named conflict procedure — e.g., generate a starter charter from quiz answers (fits Commons' existing House Ledger).
- **[suggestive]** The most-cited practitioner estimate is that ~90% of forming intentional communities fail, with failures attributed primarily to interpersonal and 'structural' conflict — absent vision documents, unclear agreements, and undefined decision-making — rather than land or money.
  - *Source:* Christian (2003), 'Creating a Life Together: Practical Tools to Grow Ecovillages and Intentional Communities', New Society Publishers — practitioner synthesis, base rate not peer-reviewed; marked suggestive for that reason
  - *Design implication:* HARD FILTER: agreement on how decisions get made. A house that never decided how it decides is the canonical failure. Include one forced-choice item on preferred decision style (consensus / delegated roles / majority vote / informal) and treat large mismatches as near-dealbreakers rather than averaging them into a %.
- **[suggestive]** A meta-analysis of ecovillage case studies found the difficulty of consensus-based decision-making to be a top internal challenge and a main reason members leave.
  - *Source:* 'A framework to identify and develop potential ecovillages: Meta-analysis from the studies of world's ecovillages', Sustainable Cities and Society (c. 2019, ScienceDirect S2210670718308333) — venue verified, authors not verified, hence suggestive
  - *Design implication:* Don't assume consensus = good. Measure tolerance for meetings/process explicitly ('how many hours/month of house meetings are acceptable to you?') — process-hungry and process-averse people burning each other out is a documented exit driver, making this a SIMILARITY dimension with hard-filter tails.
- **[moderate]** Strict groups are strong because demandingness screens out free riders and raises average participation — members who accept entry costs contribute disproportionately more.
  - *Source:* Iannaccone (1994), 'Why Strict Churches Are Strong', American Journal of Sociology 99(5):1180-1211 — well-known citation, verified from prior knowledge (not re-fetched this session), theory influential and empirically supported in denominational data though contested
  - *Design implication:* MAIN EFFECT via self-selection: a slightly effortful quiz and joining process is a feature — completion itself signals commitment. Keep the quiz under 7 minutes, but resist making matching one-click; require reading and endorsing the house's actual obligations before a match is confirmed.
- **[suggestive]** Long-lived income-sharing and cohousing communities almost universally screen behaviorally, not psychometrically: staged pipelines of visit weekend → trial residency (e.g., Twin Oaks' ~3-week visitor period) → provisional membership (typically 6-12 months) → full membership with existing-member veto or structured feedback.
  - *Source:* Documented practice: Twin Oaks (est. 1967, still operating) membership process; Foundation for Intentional Community (ic.org) resources; recommended in Christian (2003) — practice description, not a controlled study
  - *Design implication:* The communities that actually survive treat any questionnaire as a FRONT-END to a behavioral trial. The match % should be framed as 'worth a trial' rather than a verdict, and the product should scaffold the trial itself: structured visit checklist, defined trial-stay length, and a built-in 30/60-day mutual review with an easy no-fault exit.

### Usable instruments

- **Costly-Requirements / Willingness-to-Commit checklist (adapted from Sosis & Bressler's 22-constraint coding scheme and Kanter's commitment mechanisms)** — 8-12 binary or 4-point items ('would happily accept' → 'dealbreaker'), ~2 minutes. Construct: Commitment level: which concrete household obligations (weekly shared meal, chore hours, meeting attendance, guest limits, shared purchases, quiet hours) a person will actually accept. Directly operationalizes the strongest longevity literature; doubles as a SIMILARITY dimension (match people at the same commitment tier) and auto-extracts HARD FILTERS (any 'dealbreaker' response). Not a validated psychometric scale — it is a structured preference elicitation, which is honest and appropriate here.
- **Ostrom Design Principles group assessment (Prosocial adaptation; Atkins, Wilson & Hayes 2019, 'Prosocial')** — 8 core-design-principle statements rated on 5-point Likert (one per principle), ~1.5 minutes; longer validated Prosocial survey versions exist. Construct: Governance health of an existing house: boundary clarity, fair contribution rules, voice in decisions, monitoring, graduated sanctions, fast conflict resolution. Backed by the strongest evidence in this area (Cox et al. 2010 meta-analytic support for the principles). Use it on the HOUSE side of the marketplace — candidates deserve to see a governance-health score, and it nudges houses to formalize agreements. Adaptation to households is an extrapolation; label it as such.
- **Brief Sense of Community Scale (BSCS)** — 8 items, 5-point Likert, <1 minute. Construct: Sense of community: needs fulfillment, membership, influence, shared emotional connection (McMillan & Chavis framework). Peterson, Speer & McMillan (2008, Journal of Community Psychology) — properly validated and very short. Best used NOT for matching but as the product's outcome metric: administer to housemates at 60/180 days to validate and retrain the match algorithm against something real.
- **Decision-style and process-tolerance forced-choice block (derived from ecovillage exit research and Christian 2003)** — 3-4 forced-choice items (preferred decision method; acceptable meeting hours/month; response to a broken agreement), <1 minute. Construct: Governance preference and process tolerance. Mismatched decision-style expectations and consensus fatigue are documented leading exit drivers; forced-choice keeps it fast and the extreme-mismatch cells can be scored as near-dealbreakers instead of averaged away. Author-constructed, not validated — flag for future criterion validation via BSCS outcomes.

## 5 · Money & values compatibility

Money is the single best-documented conflict domain in shared-life research: financial disagreements predict relationship dissolution better than any other disagreement topic, and diary studies show money conflicts are uniquely recurrent, intense, and likely to stay unresolved — so a matching quiz should screen money compatibility ex ante rather than assume houses will "work it out." The evidence sorts cleanly into the three design buckets. MAIN EFFECTS: prosocial Social Value Orientation predicts cooperation in group public-goods situations (two meta-analyses), while materialism predicts perceived financial problems and lower relationship quality even when both parties are equally materialistic — so prosociality and low materialism are traits to score per-person, not to match on likeness. SIMILARITY: value similarity (Schwartz-type values) predicts satisfaction where personality similarity does not, and person-group value congruence is a meta-analytically strong predictor of group satisfaction and commitment — plus mismatched fairness norms (strict 50/50 score-keeping vs. communal vs. need-based cost-sharing) are a distinct, measurable friction axis. HARD FILTERS: the heavily replicated Family Stress Model shows economic pressure itself spills into interpersonal conflict, justifying a rent-affordability/financial-strain screen, and extreme financial secrecy preferences deserve a disclosure flag. One caveat: most of this evidence comes from married/romantic couples and workgroups; direct housemate studies are sparse (the closest, Murstein & Azar 1986, supports the exchange-orientation risk), so couple-to-housemate generalization should be treated as a design assumption and validated with Commons' own outcome data.

### Findings

- **[moderate]** In a national longitudinal sample of 4,574 couples, frequency of financial disagreements predicted divorce more strongly than any other disagreement topic (sex, in-laws, chores), and the effect held after controlling for income, debt, and net worth.
  - *Source:* Dew, Britt & Huston (2012), 'Examining the Relationship Between Financial Issues and Divorce', Family Relations 61 — verified via Wiley/BYU ScholarsArchive
  - *Design implication:* Money deserves its own quiz module rather than one item, and raw income should NOT be the matching variable — expected money-conflict behavior (disagreement frequency, spending style) is the signal. For existing houses evaluating an applicant, a direct 'how often do you end up in disagreements about money with people you live with' item is evidence-backed.
- **[moderate]** In a diary study of 100 couples logging 748 home conflicts, money conflicts were not the most frequent but were more pervasive, problematic, and recurrent than non-money conflicts, and more likely to remain unresolved despite more problem-solving attempts.
  - *Source:* Papp, Cummings & Goeke-Morey (2009), 'For Richer, for Poorer: Money as a Topic of Marital Conflict in the Home', Family Relations 58, 91-103 — verified via PubMed/Wiley
  - *Design implication:* Because money conflicts resist resolution once they start, weight money-practice mismatches more heavily in the match score than mismatches on topics that resolve easily (e.g., noise, decor) — prevention via matching beats in-house conflict resolution for this domain specifically.
- **[strong]** Prosocial Social Value Orientation reliably predicts actual cooperation in social dilemmas and public-goods games (r ≈ .30), confirmed by two independent meta-analyses (82 studies in the 2009 meta).
  - *Source:* Balliet, Parks & Joireman (2009), Group Processes & Intergroup Relations; replicated/extended by Pletzer, Balliet, Joireman, Kuhlman, Voelpel & Van Lange (2018), European Journal of Personality — both verified via Crossref
  - *Design implication:* This is a MAIN EFFECT, not a similarity dimension: prosocials contribute more to household public goods (cleaning, shared supplies, house funds) with anyone. Score SVO per-person as a 'good housemate' trait and treat strongly individualistic/competitive orientations as a soft caution flag, not a matching axis.
- **[moderate]** Higher materialism predicts greater perceived financial problems and lower relationship satisfaction, and materialism matters more for perceived financial problems than actual income (N = 600 married couples, SEM).
  - *Source:* Dean, Carroll & Yang (2007), 'Materialism, Perceived Financial Problems, and Marital Satisfaction', Family and Consumer Sciences Research Journal 35 — verified via Wiley/BYU ScholarsArchive
  - *Design implication:* Ask about spending values and money attitudes, not income brackets: two housemates with identical rent budgets can have very different perceived financial strain, and it is the perception that drives conflict.
- **[moderate]** In a couple-profiles study of 1,734 couples, materialism was negatively associated with relationship quality even when both partners were congruently high in materialism — similarity did not neutralize the risk.
  - *Source:* Carroll, Dean, Call & Busby (~2011), 'Materialism and Marriage: Couple Profiles of Congruent and Incongruent Spouses' — title/authors verified via BYU ScholarsArchive; journal/year (Journal of Couple & Relationship Therapy, 2011) not independently confirmed
  - *Design implication:* This is direct evidence AGAINST naive 'match on likeness for everything': materialism is a main-effect risk factor, so do not boost match % for two high-materialism applicants — treat high materialism as a per-person risk input instead.
- **[moderate]** Among couples, similarity in personal values (Schwartz values) predicted relationship satisfaction while similarity in Big Five personality traits did not.
  - *Source:* Leikas, Ilmarinen, Verkasalo, Vartiainen & Lönnqvist (2018), 'Relationship satisfaction and similarity of personality traits, personal values, and attitudes', Personality and Individual Differences — verified via Crossref
  - *Design implication:* If quiz time forces a choice between measuring personality similarity and values similarity, spend the minutes on values: match on Schwartz-type value profiles (e.g., self-transcendence vs. self-enhancement, tradition vs. openness) rather than trait likeness.
- **[strong]** Meta-analysis of 172 workplace studies shows person-group and person-organization value congruence strongly predict satisfaction and commitment (ρ = .44 for satisfaction, .51 for commitment, -.35 for intent to quit), generalizing across situations.
  - *Source:* Kristof-Brown, Zimmerman & Johnson (2005), 'Consequences of Individuals' Fit at Work', Personnel Psychology 58, 281-342 — verified via Wiley/Crossref
  - *Design implication:* Match the applicant against the HOUSE's aggregate value profile (person-group congruence), not just pairwise against each member — a house should have a computed value centroid and the quiz should score distance from it. Fit also predicts staying, which maps directly to housemate turnover, a metric Commons can track.
- **[suggestive]** Exchange orientation — expecting strict, tracked reciprocity in benefits — was associated with LOWER roommate compatibility, consistent with the established communal-vs-exchange norms framework of Clark & Mills.
  - *Source:* Murstein & Azar (1986), 'The Relationship of Exchange-Orientation to Friendship Intensity, Roommate Compatibility, Anxiety, and Friendship', Small Group Behavior 17 — existence verified via Crossref; single dated study, so marked suggestive. Framework: Clark & Mills (1979), JPSP
  - *Design implication:* The rare directly-on-roommates money finding: measure preferred cost-sharing norm (strict itemized splitting and prompt repayment vs. rough communal flow). Norm MISMATCH between housemates is friction, and very high score-keeping orientation may itself be a main-effect risk worth a soft flag.
- **[suggestive]** Deutsch's classic distributive-justice theory distinguishes three fairness norms — equity (proportional to contribution), equality (same for all), and need — and predicts that groups applying mismatched allocation norms experience perceived injustice; direct tests in shared households are sparse.
  - *Source:* Deutsch (1975), 'Equity, Equality, and Need: What Determines Which Value Will Be Used as the Basis of Distributive Justice?', Journal of Social Issues 31 — classic, widely cited; application to housemates is theoretical extrapolation
  - *Design implication:* Use 2-3 concrete scenario items to classify applicants as equality/equity/need allocators (e.g., 'rooms differ in size — split rent evenly, by square footage, or by what each person can afford?'; 'one housemate earns 3x the others — groceries split how?'). Sharp allocator-type mismatches should lower match % and be surfaced as a discussion prompt, not hidden in the score.
- **[moderate]** Financial infidelity — hiding spending, purchases, or accounts from a partner — is common, measurable, and anticipating the partner's disapproval drives concealment behavior, with secrecy harming relationship outcomes.
  - *Source:* Garbinsky, Gladstone, Nikolova & Olson (2020, online 2019), 'Love, Lies, and Money: Financial Infidelity in Romantic Relationships', Journal of Consumer Research 47 — verified via Crossref
  - *Design implication:* Add a financial-transparency expectations item: comfort with a shared visible house ledger and disclosed contributions (this dovetails with Commons' existing House Ledger feature). Mismatch between a transparency-expecting house and a privacy-preferring applicant predicts concealment dynamics; strong secrecy preference in a pooled-expenses house is a soft filter.
- **[strong]** The Family Stress Model — economic pressure produces psychological distress which spills into interpersonal conflict and relationship deterioration — has been replicated across decades, samples, and countries.
  - *Source:* Conger et al. (1990s originals); review: Masarik & Conger (2017), 'Stress and child development: a review of the Family Stress Model', Current Opinion in Psychology 13 — verified via Crossref
  - *Design implication:* This justifies a HARD FILTER that is about strain, not wealth: screen rent-to-income burden or subjective financial strain (e.g., CFPB scale). An applicant for whom the rent is a severe stretch is a conflict risk in ANY house regardless of values fit — better handled as an affordability gate than folded into the match %.
- **[suggestive]** Money-compatibility evidence is overwhelmingly derived from married/romantic couples and workgroups, not housemates; housemate-specific validation is thin.
  - *Source:* Synthesis across the sources above (Dew 2012; Papp 2009; Kristof-Brown 2005; Murstein & Azar 1986 as nearest housemate analog)
  - *Design implication:* Present the redesigned quiz honestly as 'built on couple and small-group research, adapted for co-living', and instrument the platform to collect its own criterion data (housemate turnover, ledger disputes, weekly check-in conflict flags) so weights can be re-fit — Commons is positioned to generate the missing housemate-specific evidence.

### Usable instruments

- **SVO Slider Measure (Murphy, Ackermann & Handgraaf, 2011, Judgment and Decision Making)** — 6 primary items; each is a slider choosing among 9 self/other money allocations; ~2 minutes; yields a continuous 'SVO angle' classifying altruistic/prosocial/individualistic/competitive. Construct: Social Value Orientation — dispositional prosociality in resource allocation. Meta-analytically validated predictor of real cooperation in public-goods situations (household chores, shared funds); excellent test-retest reliability (r = .92); behavioral-choice format resists faking better than agree/disagree items; free to use; allocations can be reskinned in house-budget framing ('you get $85, your housemate gets $85' style). Score as a MAIN-EFFECT trait, not a similarity match.
- **Short Schwartz's Value Survey (SSVS; Lindeman & Verkasalo, 2005, Journal of Personality Assessment)** — 10 items — one per Schwartz value (e.g., Benevolence, Power, Tradition), each rated for importance as a life-guiding principle on a 9-point scale; ~2 minutes. Construct: Schwartz's 10 basic personal values, reducible to the two higher-order axes (self-transcendence vs. self-enhancement; openness vs. conservation). The fastest validated route to a value profile, and value similarity is the similarity dimension that actually predicts relationship satisfaction (unlike personality similarity). Compute applicant-to-house-centroid distance on the two higher-order axes for the SIMILARITY component of the match score.
- **Material Values Scale — Short Form (MVS-9; Richins, 2004, Journal of Consumer Research)** — 9 items (5-point Likert agree/disagree); 6- and 3-item versions exist but the 9-item is the shortest with acceptable general-level psychometrics; ~1.5 minutes. Construct: Materialism (possessions as central to life, as success signal, and as happiness source). Materialism predicts perceived financial problems and relationship strain as a MAIN EFFECT — even in congruent pairs — so a 3-6 item subset flags a money-conflict risk factor that likeness-matching would wrongly reward. If time-pressed, the validated 3- or 6-item cut can be used with a noted reliability tradeoff.
- **CFPB Financial Well-Being Scale — Short Form (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, 2015/2017 technical report)** — 5 items (Likert; e.g., 'I am just getting by financially'), IRT-scored with published lookup tables; public domain; ~1 minute. Construct: Subjective financial well-being / financial strain. Government-developed, nationally normed, free instrument for the HARD-FILTER side: per the Family Stress Model, financial strain predicts interpersonal conflict in any house. Use as an affordability/strain gate (possibly combined with a rent-to-income question) rather than as a match-percentage input, and handle sensitively — it screens strain, not wealth.
- **Communal/Exchange orientation items (classic: Clark, Ouellette, Powell & Milberg 1987 Communal Orientation Scale, 14 items; modern target-specific version: Exchange and Communal Orientations (ECO) scale, PLOS ONE 2025) — adapt 3-4 items or use Deutsch-style allocation scenarios** — Full scales are 14+ Likert items; recommend a 3-4 item adaptation or 2-3 forced-choice cost-splitting scenarios (equality vs. equity vs. need) to stay in time budget; ~1 minute. Construct: Preferred fairness/reciprocity norm for shared resources: strict score-keeping reciprocity vs. communal need-responsiveness; allocation norm type. Fairness-norm MISMATCH is the money-specific similarity axis the couple literature and the one direct roommate study (Murstein & Azar 1986) both point at, and it is invisible to generic value or personality measures; scenario items ('split rent for unequal rooms how?') double as post-match discussion prompts the house can review before accepting someone. Weakest validation pedigree of the five in short form — treat scores as conversation-starters plus a mismatch penalty, and validate against Commons outcome data.
